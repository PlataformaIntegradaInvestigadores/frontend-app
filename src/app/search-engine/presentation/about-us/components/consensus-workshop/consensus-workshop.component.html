Consensus Workshop

Consensus System for Researchers

The core idea behind having a consensus mediation system like CENTINELA stems from the needs of researchers and academics to streamline their decision-making processes. CENTINELA acts as a facilitator, guiding research teams in reaching an agreement on which research topic work in group. The system generates a curated list of five potential research topics, allowing users to engage interactively with each option. Through this collaborative interaction, the platform helps researchers converge on the most suitable topic that aligns with the interests and expertise of the group, making the decision-making process more efficient and focused.

What is consensus workshop?

It is a creative planning method, useful for creating action plans, making decisions, and generating options in both normal and conflict situations. It allows the collection of ideas, the establishment of general guidelines, the incorporation of different perspectives, and the achievement of a consensual solution among the parties.

CENTINELA on consensus workshop

It was developed in three distinct phases, each designed to streamline the decision-making process.

Phase One: Its purpose is to familiarize participants with the proposed research topics and expand their understanding of the available options.

Phase Two: Here, the group members actively engage with the topics, ranking them according to their personal interests and preferences.

Phase Tree: The system presents the consensus results based on the researchers' rankings and preferences.

Phase 1 : Presentation

In the first phase, once the group is formed, the system assigns five research topics for the group to explore. The goal of this phase is to help users become familiar with the topics they may work on collaboratively. This ensures that all group members have a clear understanding of the available options before proceeding to the next stages of the consensus process.

Topic Exploration via Google Scholar: Users can explore more information about each topic by being redirected to Google Scholar, allowing them to gain a deeper understanding of the research areas.

Phase 2 : Valuation

The second phase focuses on allowing each user to re-rank the research topics presented in Phase One based on their individual preferences. This phase ensures that every group member can express their priorities, which helps guide the overall consensus-building process. Users organize the topics in order of interest, with the top position representing the topic that most appeals to them.

Re-Ranking of Topics: Users are tasked with ranking the five research topics according to their personal preferences. The first position is reserved for the topic that interests the user the most, with the remaining topics ranked in descending order of interest.

Topic Tagging with Predefined Labels: Users can label each topic with one of five predefined tags to further categorize the topics. Three tags are positive Novel, Attractive, and Trending while two are negative Obsolete and Unfamiliar. When a topic is labeled Novel, it is automatically moved to the top of the list, while a topic tagged as Obsolete is placed at the bottom.

Phase 3 : Decision

In the final phase, the results of the consensus are presented to the group in a podium format, where the top-ranked research topic is awarded the "gold medal," signifying that it is the most suitable topic for the team to pursue.

Podium Display of Results: The research topic ranked highest by the group is placed at the top, symbolized with a gold medal, followed by the second and third-ranked topics. This visual hierarchy helps the group quickly identify the most favored research direction based on their collective input

Algorithms for Consensus Workshop

Positional Algorithms:

Positional algorithms assign scores to each option based on their ranking in voters' preferences. Options ranked higher receive more points. The result is a weighted average influenced by participants' expertise levels.

Formula:
Weighted Score = Σ (Positionuser × Expertiseuser) / Σ (Expertiseuser)

This formula ensures that participants with higher expertise have a proportionally greater influence on the final decision.

Schulze Method (Non-Positional):

The Schulze method identifies the strongest paths between options based on pairwise comparisons. It evaluates the dominance of one option over another by considering all possible paths.

Key Steps:
1. Create a pairwise comparison matrix P for all options.
2. Calculate the path strength matrix S using the Floyd-Warshall algorithm:
S[i][j] = max(S[i][j], min(S[i][k], S[k][j])) (for all options k).
3. Rank options by their total dominance.

This method is particularly effective in contexts with complex or non-linear preferences.
